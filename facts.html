All Facts

1. Orion is one of the brightest constellations.

Orion is one of the brightest and most recognizable constellations in the night sky. Its three famous stars, known as Orion’s Belt, make it easy to find, while Betelgeuse and Rigel shine as two of the brightest stars in our sky. Many cultures around the world have told stories about Orion, making it one of the most widely recognized constellations in human history.

2. Ursa Major contains the Big Dipper.

Ursa Major, or the Great Bear, is a large constellation in the northern sky that contains the famous Big Dipper. The Dipper’s “bowl” stars point toward Polaris, the North Star, which has helped travelers and sailors find their way for centuries. While the Big Dipper is only part of Ursa Major, it’s often the first shape people notice in the sky.

3. Pisces represents two fish tied together by a cord.

Pisces is a zodiac constellation symbolizing two fish connected by a cord, said to represent Aphrodite and Eros escaping the monster Typhon in Greek mythology. Though its stars are faint, Pisces plays an important role in astrology and has been observed for thousands of years.

4. Scorpius looks like a scorpions curved tail.

Scorpius is a constellation that truly lives up to its name, with its stars forming a long curve that resembles a scorpion’s tail. At its center lies Antares, a giant red star often called the “heart of the scorpion.” In Greek mythology, Scorpius was the creature that defeated Orion, and the two constellations appear on opposite sides of the sky as if to continue their eternal chase.

5. Cassiopeia is shaped like a W in the night sky.

Cassiopeia is a constellation that forms a striking “W” shape, making it one of the easiest constellations to recognize. Named after a vain queen in Greek mythology, it is visible year-round in the Northern Hemisphere. Its unique shape and cultural stories make it a favorite among stargazers.

6. Lyra contains Vega, one of the brghtest stars.

Lyra, the lyre or harp, is a small constellation that contains Vega, one of the brightest stars in the sky. Vega forms part of the Summer Triangle, a famous asterism seen in summer. In mythology, Lyra represents the harp of Orpheus, a legendary musician whose music could charm all living things.

7. Cygnus is also called the Northern Cross.

Cygnus, the swan, stretches across the Milky Way and is also known as the Northern Cross because of its cross-like shape. Its brightest star, Deneb, forms another corner of the Summer Triangle. Cygnus is home to many deep-sky wonders, making it popular with astronomers and stargazers alike.

8. Leo represents the Nermean Lion fro Greek mythology.

Leo, one of the zodiac constellations, symbolizes the Nemean Lion defeated by Hercules in his twelve labors. Its sickle-shaped stars resemble a lion’s mane, and Regulus, its brightest star, shines at the lion’s heart. Leo dominates spring skies in the Northern Hemisphere.

9. Taurus holds the famous Pleiades star cluster.

Taurus, the bull, is home to the famous Pleiades star cluster, also known as the Seven Sisters. Another highlight is Aldebaran, a bright red giant star marking the bull’s fiery eye. In Greek mythology, Taurus is linked to Zeus, who disguised himself as a bull.

10. Gemini represents the twins Castor and Pollux.

Gemini, another zodiac constellation, represents the mythological twins Castor and Pollux, whose names are given to its two brightest stars. They symbolize brotherhood and companionship, and the constellation is most visible in winter skies.

11. Aquila means "eagle" and features the brightest star Altair.

Aquila, the eagle, is a constellation whose brightest star, Altair, is part of the Summer Triangle. In mythology, Aquila was the eagle of Zeus, carrying his thunderbolts. Altair’s brightness and location near the Milky Way make it easy to spot in summer.

12. Pegasus is known for the Great Square asterism.

Pegasus, the winged horse, is one of the largest constellations in the sky. Its most famous feature is the Great Square of Pegasus, a large rectangle of stars that helps astronomers find nearby constellations like Andromeda.

13. Andromeda contains the closest spiral galaxy to the Milky Way.

Andromeda is famous for containing the Andromeda Galaxy, the closest spiral galaxy to our own. In mythology, Andromeda was a princess saved from a sea monster by Perseus. The constellation stretches across the autumn sky in the Northern Hemisphere.

14. Draco winds between Ursa Major and Ursa Minor

Draco, the dragon, coils between the Big Dipper and the Little Dipper in the northern sky. In mythology, it often represents Ladon, the dragon who guarded the golden apples of the Hesperides. Despite its faintness, Draco’s winding shape is striking.

15. Sagittarius is home to the center of our galaxy.

Sagittarius, the archer, points toward the center of the Milky Way. Its “teapot” asterism makes it easy to find in summer skies. In mythology, Sagittarius is depicted as a centaur with a bow and arrow aimed at Scorpius.

16. Canis Major contains Sirius, the brightest star in the sky.

Canis Major, or the Great Dog, is home to Sirius, the brightest star visible from Earth. Often called the Dog Star, Sirius is so bright it can be seen even from cities. The constellation follows Orion across the heavens as his loyal hunting companion.

17. Capricornus is associated with a sea-goat in mythology.

Capricornus, or Capricorn, is a zodiac constellation representing a creature that is half goat and half fish. Though its stars are faint, Capricornus has been symbolically important in astrology and ancient cultures for thousands of years.

18. Hydra is the largest constellation in the sky.

Hydra, the water serpent, is the longest constellation, stretching across more than 100 degrees of the sky. It contains Alphard, its brightest star, whose name means “the solitary one.” In mythology, Hydra was the many-headed serpent slain by Hercules.

19. Virgo contains Spica, a bright blue-white star.

Virgo, one of the zodiac constellations, represents a maiden and contains Spica, a bright blue-white star that marks her hand. Virgo is one of the largest constellations and is prominent in spring skies. In mythology, Virgo is often linked to fertility and harvest.

20. Libra is the only zodiac constellation symbolized by an object, the scales.

Libra, the scales, is unique among the zodiac constellations because it represents balance and justice rather than a living creature. Its brightest stars form the image of a set of scales, long associated with fairness and law.
